# March 2026 Sell Candidates

*The March offseason window captures different types of sell opportunities: players coming off peak seasons whose value will only decline from here, players in situations that cap their ceiling, and injury concerns who still carry residual name-value. The common thread is that now — before training camp and draft news reshape the market — is when you extract maximum value.*

*The March offseason is the best selling window of the year. Managers evaluate rosters with fresh eyes and often pay a slight premium for recognizable names before training camp reports and depth chart news arrive in July.*

## Quarterback

### Anthony Richardson, Indianapolis Colts — SELL

Richardson's dynasty arc was one of the most frustrating stories of 2025. He lost the Colts' starting job before Week 1, appeared in only 2 games with 1 completion, and in October suffered a fractured orbital bone that ended his season on IR. This is no longer the "flashes of upside but injury concerns" narrative that dynasty managers have been rationalizing for three seasons — this is a player who has appeared in a combined handful of games across three NFL years, never establishing himself as a starter. He has now missed the effective entirety of three NFL seasons.

The Colts' patience is visibly fraying. Indianapolis has 2026 draft capital that could reasonably be used on a quarterback. Richardson is only 23, which is why his market value hasn't fully collapsed — buyers still hold onto the arm-talent dream. That residual optimism is your exit window. Move him before the next injury news arrives.

**In Superflex specifically, this sell signal is amplified.** The format pays a premium for any young QB with upside. Use that Superflex market to extract a skill-position asset while buyers still see the ceiling.

## Running Back

### Jonathan Taylor, Indianapolis Colts — SELL HIGH

Taylor turned 27 in January 2026 and his 2025 season was arguably the best of his career: **1,585 rushing yards, 18 rushing TDs (20 total), 4.9 yards per carry** — an MVP-caliber performance that placed him among the top-five RB seasons in recent memory. He was dominant.

That's exactly why you sell now.

The dynasty community is slow to recognize that peak performance often marks the sell window, not the buy window. Taylor turns 28 in September 2026. The dynasty age cliff for running backs arrives precisely at 28. The gap between "what he just did" and "what he'll do next" is at its widest right now — and every potential buyer has just watched his stat line. You will not find a higher market price than the one that exists today.

**This is a classic sell-high.** Peak age, peak production, maximum perceived value in trade conversations. The window is now.

### Javonte Williams, Dallas Cowboys — SELL HIGH

Williams was a dynasty darling entering 2025 — a young back with elite pre-injury efficiency who had supposedly fully recovered from his ACL tear. In 2025, signed with Dallas, he delivered the recovery: **1,201 rushing yards, 11 touchdowns, 35 receptions** — a complete, multi-phase performance.

The sell signal is about what comes next. Williams, now 26, has completed the recovery arc and is at peak perceived value. Dallas's long-term commitment to him is unclear as the Cowboys navigate cap decisions and a roster in transition. In dynasty, the player who just hit their ceiling is at maximum trade value — every buyer sees the 2025 stat line and projects it forward. Sell into that projection before depth chart competition, injury risk, or a scheme change resets the market.

The sell window on a fully-recovered back posting career numbers is narrower than it looks. Move him now.

## Wide Receiver

### Drake London, Atlanta Falcons — SELL

London is the cautionary tale of waiting for a receiver who has never had a consistent quarterback. After four NFL seasons, he has produced one genuinely good fantasy year (2023) surrounded by mediocrity. The Falcons' offensive continuity issues in 2025 — Michael Penix Jr. struggled in his first full starting season, the offensive coordinator was fired after Week 10 — created another environment where London's raw talent went underutilized.

He finished with 68 receptions, 919 yards, and 7 touchdowns in 12 games — decent per-game rate but limited by missed time. He is 25 and entering the theoretical prime of his career, but the situation shows no signs of improving. Dynasty managers have been "waiting on the situation to stabilize" for three years. That's the tell.

You can likely sell him for more than he's worth based on his 2021 draft pedigree. Take it.

### Zay Flowers, Baltimore Ravens — HOLD / SELL (contenders)

Flowers had a career year in 2025 — **86 receptions, 1,211 yards, and 6 touchdowns** — and is now at maximum perceived value heading into his age-25 season. He is firmly in the WR2 conversation.

The dynasty question is whether this is a new baseline or a career high that won't repeat. Baltimore's offensive design works against any single receiver exceeding 100 targets in a season — Lamar Jackson's rushing floor, the run-first red-zone philosophy, and the committee passing approach create a structural ceiling on Flowers's volume. His 2025 line was excellent; it may also represent his peak.

Rebuilders with patience should hold — the 25-year-old upside is real. **Contenders who need production now** face a different calculus: you can extract maximum value right now based on the 2025 career-high stat line. If you're a contender evaluating your WR room, the window to sell at peak value is open. The system ceiling is already visible; wait for regression and that window closes.

## Tight End

### Sam LaPorta, Detroit Lions — SELL (contenders) / BUY-LOW (rebuilders)

LaPorta's 2023 rookie breakout (86/889) looked like the beginning of an elite dynasty career. 2025 was a significant correction. He dealt with a back injury that required surgery, limiting him to 9 games, and his final line — **40 receptions and 489 yards** — represented a steep regression from the expectations his rookie year had set.

He turns 25 in 2026 and there is legitimate reason to believe the knee situation resolves. However, the Lions' scheme concern is real: they will never be a high-target environment for the TE position. His ceiling in Detroit may simply be lower than his 2024 line suggested.

**Contenders should sell at the post-2025 depressed price.** Rebuilders who can buy low and wait two seasons for a bounce-back may find value — but buy with eyes open to the scheme ceiling.

## The Common Thread

These six players share a pattern: their current trade prices reflect **residual name-value and prior narrative**, not underlying trajectory. Richardson still gets Superflex QB premium. Taylor still gets "former elite RB" premium. London still gets "2021 first-round pedigree" premium.

March is when those premiums are highest — before training camp injuries, depth chart changes, and draft news arrive and force market corrections. The window to extract full value on declining or stalled players is open **now**.

# The Best Moves in BMFFFL History

*Six seasons of dynasty football produce thousands of roster decisions. Most are forgettable. Some are the reason championships get won. These are the five best.*

---

## The Criteria

What makes a move "best"? Not just that it worked out — hindsight is cheap, and every championship team made moves that look smart in retrospect. The best moves satisfy a stricter standard:

1. **It was non-obvious at the time.** Any move that seemed good in the moment and then was good isn't interesting. The best moves are the ones where you had to see something others didn't.
2. **It was actionable — someone had to actually make it.** Information is useless without execution. The move had to be executed cleanly.
3. **It had a measurable impact on competitive outcomes.** Not just "the player was good" but "this specific decision affected playoff results."

With that framework, here are the five best moves in BMFFFL history.

---

## #1: MLSchools12's Superflex QB Architecture (League-Wide, 2020-2021)

This is less a single move and more a philosophy that became a structural advantage, but it belongs first because it's the foundation of the most successful dynasty in BMFFFL history.

When the league adopted the Superflex format, the mathematical implication was immediate: having two elite quarterbacks in a scoring format with 4-point passing TDs and a superflex spot multiplies QB value dramatically. An owner who correctly identified this — earlier than the rest of the field — and rostered two legitimate QB1s gained a weekly floor that couldn't be matched by owners starting a running back or wide receiver in the flex.

MLSchools12 built their 2021 championship roster around exactly this principle. The combination of Lamar Jackson and Kyler Murray as the 1-2 SF quarterback pair created a weekly scoring floor that made their team nearly impossible to out-score in an average matchup. When both QBs were healthy and producing, the starting lineup had a point total ceiling that forced opponents into variance-seeking desperation.

The 150-103 championship blowout over rbr in 2021 was the proof of concept. rbr was a well-constructed roster. They just couldn't match the QB surplus.

**Why it's #1**: Every other smart move in this list was a reaction to the market. The superflex QB philosophy wasn't a reaction — it was MLSchools12 recognizing, before anyone else acted on it consistently, that the format rewarded a specific construction. The .819 career win percentage, the 68-15 record, and the two championships trace back to this decision.

## #2: JuicyBussy Acquires CMC (Timing Unknown, Impact 2023)

Christian McCaffrey is the rare dynasty asset that makes your roster categorically different when he's healthy. At peak CMC — which the 2023 NFL season provided — you have a running back scoring 20+ fantasy points per game in PPR formats with elite receptions volume. In a Full PPR league like BMFFFL, where every reception scores 1.0 points, CMC is the single most valuable non-QB dynasty asset.

JuicyBussy had McCaffrey. He was the centerpiece of the 2023 championship run.

The 2023 BMFFFL season saw JuicyBussy enter the playoffs as the 6th seed with an 8-6 record. They proceeded to beat three consecutive opponents, each seeded higher. That does not happen without elite-level production from a player who can post multiple touchdowns and double-digit receptions in the same game. McCaffrey provided the weekly ceiling that made each upset possible.

The acquisition of CMC — whenever it happened in the dynasty timeline — was the move that opened the championship window. Without it, 2023 JuicyBussy is a .500 team that makes the playoffs and exits in the first round. With it, they're champions.

**Why it's #2**: McCaffrey in full PPR dynasty is a known commodity. But knowing a player is elite and actually rostering them — paying the price, holding through injury, building the rest of the lineup around the certainty of his production — requires execution. JuicyBussy executed it. The 2023 championship is the return.

## #3: JuicyBussy Adds D'Andre Swift as a Late-Season FA (2023)

This is the move that separates good dynasty managers from great ones. In season management — recognizing a player whose value has been underestimated by the waiver wire and acting before the rest of the league catches on — is where championships are won or lost at the margin.

D'Andre Swift in 2023 was a late-season free agency add that gave JuicyBussy roster depth at exactly the right moment. Swift's role in the Philadelphia Eagles offense was expanding in the second half of the season. The target volume and rushing work were trending upward while the fantasy community was still processing his transition from Detroit.

Adding Swift as a playoff-week contributor meant that JuicyBussy entered the postseason with a real two-pronged backfield option. The combined floor of McCaffrey (starter, ceiling) and Swift (depth, reliable PPR back) meant that even if McCaffrey had an uncharacteristically quiet game, the roster had a legitimate backup at the second-most valuable position in the format.

**Why it's #3**: Late-season FA adds during a championship run are the kind of move that gets forgotten when the trophy is handed out. People remember the championship, not the November waiver pickup that secured it. But Swift's add was the difference between JuicyBussy having one elite RB and having genuine depth at the position that wins Full PPR championships. In a format where every reception counts, having two high-target running backs in your starting lineup is the formula.

## #4: Grandes' Patient Rebuild (2020-2022)

This one is different from the others because it isn't a single transaction. It's a three-year philosophy.

Grandes went 4-9 in the 2020 inaugural season. By dynasty standards, 4-9 in year one should be expected — you're drafting into an unknown league, learning the format, figuring out what the superflex actually means. Most owners responded to a bad 2020 by making reactive trades and chasing win-now pieces.

Grandes did not do that. Instead, the 2021 season (10-4, playoff exit round one) and 2022 regular season (8-6) showed a roster that was building steadily. They weren't dominant. They weren't losing. They were accumulating.

Then the 2022 playoffs happened. Grandes, as the 4th seed, went through the bracket and beat rbr 137-115 in the championship. The championship roster wasn't built for 2022 specifically — it was built over three years of patient accumulation and matured simultaneously.

**Why it's #4**: In dynasty football, the easiest bad decision is the panic trade. When you're 4-9, the pressure to do something — anything — to improve is intense. Grandes resisted it. The 2022 championship is the direct result of three years of resisting the panic trade and letting the dynasty build properly. It's the single best example in BMFFFL history of long-term thinking paying off.

## #5: MLSchools12 Staying the Course After 2020 Semi Exit (2020-2021)

This is the psychological move, and it matters as much as any transaction.

In 2020, MLSchools12 went 11-2 with 2,115 fantasy points — the best regular season by almost any measure in BMFFFL's inaugural year. They lost in the semifinals. As the best regular-season team, eliminated in the first round of the playoffs, the pressure to blow everything up and chase a different approach was real.

Instead, they returned in 2021 with the same core philosophy, refined the QB architecture, and won the championship blowing out rbr in the final.

The lesson here isn't about roster construction — it's about process. MLSchools12 had built something that worked. The semifinal exit was, in significant part, variance — a hot opponent in a single-game elimination format. Responding to variance by abandoning a correct process is the most common mistake in dynasty management. MLSchools12 didn't make it.

The 2021 championship is partly a trophy. It's also partly proof that staying the course on a correct process, even after a painful elimination, is the right call.

**Why it's #5**: Every dynasty has a moment where the process gets tested by a bad outcome. The owners who pass that test are the ones who compete consistently over six-plus seasons. The owners who fail it — who react to a single bad result by rebuilding for no structural reason — are the ones you see in the middle of the standings year after year, always making moves, never quite building something.

## Honorable Mentions

**rbr's two back-to-back runner-up builds (2021, 2022)**: Getting to two consecutive championship games requires legitimate roster quality. rbr didn't win either, but reaching both finals is an accomplishment most owners never achieve once.

**Tubes94's patient build (2021-2024)**: Joined the league in 2021, missed the playoffs the first couple of seasons, then built to back-to-back 10+ win seasons and a 2025 championship appearance. The arc mirrors the Grandes model — patience, accumulation, peak.

**tdtd19844 rebuilding from 3-11 in 2022 to champion in 2025**: If this had been a conscious, deliberate rebuild (rather than a slow build that finally peaked), it might rank in the top five. The outcome is the same: three years from worst record to championship. The arc is real.

## The Common Thread

Every move on this list shares a characteristic: the decision-maker understood something the market hadn't priced in yet. Superflex QBs before the league fully valued them. CMC as a dynasty cornerstone. Swift as a late-season add. Patient rebuilding when the market pressure is to be impatient.

Dynasty football rewards information asymmetry. The best moves are the ones where you know something your opponent doesn't, and then you act on it.

The best owners in this league — MLSchools12 by clear historical margin — make those moves consistently. That's what .819 looks like over six seasons.

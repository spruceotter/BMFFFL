In six seasons of BMFFFL, one owner has won two championships: MLSchools12. The Schoolcraft Football Team took the title in 2021 and 2024, made the championship in between, and went 13-1 in the 2025 regular season. That's not a streak — it's a standard.

The 2025 final standings tell a story that won't appear in any headline. MLSchools12 finished 13-1, the best regular season record in league history by margin. They went home without a ring. tdtd19844 won the title.

## The Paradox of 13-1

Thirteen wins in a 14-game regular season is a category of excellence that rarely produces no return. In dynasty fantasy leagues, regular season dominance is usually downstream of roster quality — better players, better depth, better decision-making. The fact that MLSchools12 went 13-1 tells you the roster was real.

The 2025 championship outcome (tdtd19844 def. Tubes94) says the playoffs happened to someone else.

This is the unfair structure of fantasy football: a team that played the best football for 13 weeks of the season can lose in two games and walk away empty-handed. It's not injustice — it's variance. But it means MLSchools12 enters 2026 with a legitimate claim to "best roster that didn't win" and a motivational chip that didn't exist when they were celebrating in 2024.

That's a six-year record of sustained excellence that no other BMFFFL owner has matched. Grandes is the only 2022 champion; JuicyBussy won in 2023; Cogdeill11 in 2020. MLSchools12 is the only owner with multiple championships.

## The Consistency Question

Sustaining a dynasty in a dynasty league is structurally different from building one.

When you're building, every decision is additive. You draft young players with upside, you make trades that absorb present-value losses for future gain, you're patient with development. The cost of being wrong is low because you have time.

When you're sustaining, you're defending value at peak. Your players are older. The league is drafting against you — everyone has studied your roster and knows your players better than they would if you were obscure. Your young players from three years ago are now expensive contracts. The draft picks you used to build are spent.

The consistency question for MLSchools12 entering 2026 isn't "are they good?" — the answer is demonstrably yes. It's "do they have the assets to stay this good for another three years, or was 2024-2025 the peak of a wave that's about to recede?"

## The 2026 Roster Signal

MLSchools12 drafted Kaytron Allen (3.01 overall) in the 2026 BMFFFL draft — a Washington running back taken 187th in the NFL draft. That's a late-round, long-shot add. Not the kind of pick you make if you think you're one player away from competing in 2026.

The 3.01 selection signals something: MLSchools12 isn't in panic mode, but they're also not investing first-round picks in near-term boosts. A 3.01 back from Washington's crowded depth chart is a 2028-at-earliest asset. It's a thoughtful low-risk add to a roster that probably doesn't need repair — it needs maintenance.

That's what a sustaining dynasty does. It doesn't swing for home runs on overpriced veterans. It collects low-cost optionality at the edges.

## The Real Competition

The most interesting story entering 2026 is that MLSchools12 is the team everyone else is measuring themselves against.

The rebuild theses — Cogdeill11's three-pick concentrated bet, YellowFever's 2028 window — they're all pointed at eventually catching a roster that has been to two championships and a 13-win season. The 2026 draft class, with its broadly shared 2027-28 maturation window, is in part a response to a field that knows what the ceiling looks like.

MLSchools12 set the standard. Whether they can hold it is the question that makes every other roster decision meaningful.

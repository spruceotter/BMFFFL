How Every BMFFFL Owner Has Evolved Over Six Seasons

Six years is enough time for a personality to emerge. Here is what six seasons tells us about each of the 12 BMFFFL franchises.

The Framework

Dynasty football is not a single season. It's a multi-year project where roster decisions compound, mistakes are expensive, and the owners who sustain success have to do something different from the ones who peak-and-crash or stay perpetually mediocre.

After six seasons, the BMFFFL has a data set substantial enough to draw real conclusions. Win percentages, playoff appearances, championship results, and roster management patterns all tell a story. This piece tells twelve of them.

MLSchools12 — The Standard

Record: 68-15 (.819) | Championships: 2 (2021, 2024) | Playoff appearances: 6/6

There is no honest way to discuss BMFFFL owner evolution without starting here. MLSchools12 is the benchmark against which every other franchise is measured, and after six seasons the gap between them and the next-best record is not small — it's 22 games.

The evolution of MLSchools12 has been one of consistent refinement rather than dramatic pivots. The 2020 season established the philosophy: build for the superflex format, prioritize QB surplus, maintain depth at every position. The semifinal exit in 2020 — as the best regular-season team — was not a reason to change course. They came back in 2021 and won convincingly.

What's interesting about the MLSchools12 arc is that the second championship (2024) required a different kind of execution than the first. By 2024, the league had largely caught up to the superflex QB philosophy. The advantage was no longer as structural — everyone understood the format. Winning in 2024 required a different edge: roster flexibility, playoff bracket awareness, and the discipline to win as the 3rd seed without the burden of being the favorite.

The ongoing challenge — the one unresolved storyline in the MLSchools12 narrative — is the top-seed playoff curse. Two 13-1 seasons. Two semifinal exits. The regular-season dominance is not translating to playoff success when the whole league is playing their best football. Whether they solve this in 2026 is the most interesting question in the BMFFFL.

SexMachineAndyD — The Perennial Contender

Record: 50-33 (.602) | Championships: 0 | Playoff appearances: 4

Second-best career win percentage in the league. Zero championships. The gap between those two facts is the defining tension of the SexMachineAndyD franchise.

Through six seasons, SexMachineAndyD has evolved into the league's most consistent non-champion. They've never had a catastrophic season. They've never fallen out of the competitive tier. They've also never closed — four playoff appearances, a runner-up in 2024, and a record that says "elite" while the trophy case says "empty."

The evolution here has been gradual maturation. Early seasons showed a franchise finding its identity — learning the format, learning the trade market, learning what a competitive dynasty looks like in a superflex 12-team setting. By 2024, they were a championship-game team. The 2024 runner-up finish to MLSchools12 (168-146) was their closest moment.

The question for the next phase of this franchise is whether they can identify what the gap is. Is it positional — a missing piece at a key position? Is it variance — just needing more playoff exposure until the bracket breaks right? Is it roster construction — a subtle misalignment between the team's strengths and what wins championships? The answer matters for 2026.

JuicyBussy — The Championship Opportunist

Record: 46-37 (.554) | Championships: 1 (2023, as #6 seed) | Playoff appearances: 5

Five playoff appearances in six seasons. A championship won from the last seed position. A dynasty resume that reads as consistently above-average with one extraordinary peak.

JuicyBussy's evolution is the story of a franchise that never quite looked dominant on paper but kept showing up in the right places. The .554 win percentage is not elite — MLSchools12 would call it a down year. But it's fifth-best in the league, and it's been sustained across six seasons, which means this is not a fluke. JuicyBussy knows how to build a competitive roster.

The 2023 championship is the defining moment. Going wire-to-wire through the bracket as the 6th seed requires winning three elimination games against higher-seeded opponents. JuicyBussy did it. The CMC and D'Andre Swift combination gave them a scoring ceiling that justified the underdog run.

The arc since 2023 is the interesting chapter. Dynasty champions often decline in the years after their peak — the players who led the run age, contracts become harder to manage, and the rest of the league adjusts. JuicyBussy has maintained playoff-caliber records since 2023, which suggests the roster has been managed well through the post-championship period. Whether the window is still fully open in 2026 is the live question.

Grandes — The Rise and Fall

Record: 42-41 (.506) | Championships: 1 (2022) | Playoff appearances: 4 | Moodie Bowl: 1 (2025)

The most dramatic arc in the BMFFFL. Grandes went from 4-9 in 2020 to champion in 2022 to last place in 2025. The entire spectrum — worst to first to worst — inside five seasons.

In the early years, Grandes showed the patience that would eventually produce the championship. The 2020 season was a learning year. 2021 was 10-4 — real improvement, playoff appearance, round-one exit. Then 2022: 8-6, 4th seed, champion. The build worked perfectly.

The post-championship arc is the cautionary tale. Every dynasty champion faces the same challenge: do you reload or do you rebuild? Grandes appears to have tried to reload — keeping the core that won in 2022 and adding around it — while that core was aging. The result was a steady decline: 9-5 in 2023 (respectable, semifinal exit), 7-7 in 2024, 4-10 and the Moodie Bowl in 2025.

Three years from champion to last place is the fastest dynasty collapse in BMFFFL history. It's also a perfectly consistent pattern with how dynasty football works. Windows open fast and close fast. The owners who survive the close are the ones who start the next build before the window fully shuts.

Grandes enters 2026 needing to do exactly what they did in 2020-2022: absorb a bad season, accumulate draft capital, and build the next cycle. The difference is they've done it before. They know what the process looks like.

rbr — The Perpetual Runner-Up

Record: 44-39 (.530) | Championships: 0 | Runner-ups: 2 (2021, 2022) | Playoff appearances: 4

Two championship game appearances in back-to-back years, and no rings. rbr is the most productive zero-championship owner in the league, and that distinction comes with a particular kind of frustration.

The 2021 and 2022 runner-up finishes happened to the same core. Twice they built a roster good enough to reach the final. Twice they lost — once badly (to MLSchools12 in 2021, 150-103), once respectably (to Grandes in 2022, 137-115). The losses are different stories: 2021 was an outmatched championship against a superior roster; 2022 was a close game that could have gone either way.

The rbr evolution since 2022 is a question mark. They've maintained four playoff appearances over six seasons, which means they haven't completely fallen off, but they haven't returned to the championship. The two-runner-up peak may have represented the franchise's roster ceiling — a team built well enough to compete but not to dominate.

What makes rbr interesting going forward is the .530 win percentage with strong roster management instincts. The fundamental building skills appear to be there. The championship-window execution is where the gap exists. In a league where winning it all requires both, that's the gap to close.

eldridsm — The Early Contender

Record: 41-42 (.494) | Championships: 0 | Runner-ups: 1 (2020) | Playoff appearances: 3

Runner-up in 2020. A strong 2023 (9-5). Significant decline in 2024-2025. eldridsm's arc is the most episodic in the league — periodic bursts of genuine competitiveness separated by quiet years.

The 2020 runner-up finish (198.34 to Cogdeill11's 203.10 in a four-point thriller) established eldridsm as a legitimate franchise. The nearly-winning score in what was the best championship game in BMFFFL history — Christmas Day Alvin Kamara and all — tells you the roster was right. One specific Kamara game changed the outcome.

The 2023 resurgence (9-5) showed that the franchise wasn't dead — the build had produced another competitive year. But 2024 (4-10) and 2025 (5-9) suggest the second window is closing the same way the first one did: without a championship.

The eldridsm evolution feels like a franchise that has strong instincts but hasn't been able to sustain a contention window long enough to win one. Twice the pieces have come together. Twice the roster has aged out before the championship moment arrived. The 2026 offseason is about whether there's a third cycle possible.

Tubes94 — The Ascending Dynasty

Record: 34-36 (.486) | Championships: 0 | Runner-ups: 1 (2025) | Joined: 2021

The youngest dynasty trajectory in the league, and the most upward-pointing one.

Tubes94 joined in 2021 and spent the first two seasons absorbing losses and building. No drama, no panic trades, no "I'm going to compete now" overcorrection. Then: 2024 (11-3), 2025 (10-4, runner-up). The two most recent seasons represent one of the steepest ascents in BMFFFL history.

What's notable about the Tubes94 evolution is the apparent absence of major mistakes. The path from expansion owner to championship finalist in five seasons suggests a clear-eyed approach to dynasty construction: draft well, be patient with young players, don't overreact to bad weeks. The .486 career win percentage is pulled down by the early learning years — the 2024-2025 record would push that percentage significantly higher.

The Tubes94 archetype entering 2026 is the most dangerous position in dynasty football: a roster that is ascending, young, battle-tested by a championship run, and not yet at its ceiling. The question is whether they can sustain the window through 2026 and beyond. History says windows close. Tubes94's roster age distribution may say it doesn't close as fast as the league average.

eldridm20 — The Inconsistent Contender

Record: 39-44 (.470) | Championships: 0 | Runner-ups: 1 (2023) | Playoff appearances: 2

Runner-up in 2023, 6-8 in each of 2024 and 2025. The volatility is the defining characteristic of the eldridm20 franchise.

The 2023 championship run was the high point — losing 179-149 to JuicyBussy was a legitimate championship performance. The roster was right for that moment. Then the regression: two consecutive 6-8 seasons, outside the playoff field.

The eldridm20 evolution suggests a franchise that peaks intermittently without a sustainable base. The swing from championship-adjacent to outside the playoffs in a single offseason is large. That kind of variance usually indicates a roster that relies heavily on one or two high-ceiling players — when those players perform well, the team is elite; when they underperform, the floor drops.

Building more durably — adding reliable contributors in addition to the peak-variance weapons — would smooth the trajectory. The 2026 offseason is an opportunity to assess whether the franchise has identified that pattern and acted on it.

Cogdeill11 — The Founding Champion Who Couldn't Repeat

Record: 38-45 (.458) | Championships: 1 (2020) | Playoff appearances: 2

Founding champion. Six seasons removed from the title. No playoff appearances since 2021. The Cogdeill11 arc is the most complete cautionary tale in the league.

In 2020, Cogdeill11 won the inaugural BMFFFL championship on Alvin Kamara's Christmas miracle. The roster was legitimate — a 10-3 regular season, a dramatic 4-point championship win. The foundation seemed real.

Then the dynasty lifecycle happened. The 2020 roster aged. Instead of rebuilding aggressively, the franchise made incremental moves — always trying to be competitive enough to make the playoffs, never quite going all-in on a full rebuild. The results speak for themselves: no playoffs from 2022 forward, sliding from 9-5 in 2021 to 4-10 in 2024.

What's revealing about the Cogdeill11 evolution is what happened to the competitive window after 2020. The original championship piece — the roster that could win — had a lifespan. The dynasty required building the next wave before the first wave crested. That didn't happen with sufficient speed.

The 1.04 pick in 2026 is a genuine opportunity to start the next cycle. Whether Cogdeill11 uses it to begin a real rebuild or to make one more incremental addition will determine the next three seasons of this franchise.

tdtd19844 — The Patient Climber

Record: 36-47 (.434) | Championships: 1 (2025) | Playoff appearances: 3

The most recent addition to the BMFFFL championship column. 3-11 in 2022. Champion in 2025. The three-year rebuild arc executed almost perfectly.

What's interesting about the tdtd19844 evolution is that it doesn't show up as impressive in the overall record — .434 career win percentage, pulled down by the early rebuild years. But the trajectory is what matters. The build accelerated. The roster peaked at the right moment. The championship arrived on schedule.

The post-championship challenge is now the central question for this franchise. No BMFFFL team has successfully defended a title. Every champion has either declined (Grandes) or remained competitive without repeating (MLSchools12 got a second ring, but with a three-year gap). The roster that won in 2025 is one year older. The rebuild teams are adding young pieces. The window is real but not infinite.

Whether tdtd19844 evolves into a dynasty contender (MLSchools12 model) or a one-and-done champion (everyone else model) will be answered over the next two to three seasons.

Cmaleski — The Steady Hand Waiting for a Break

Record: 36-47 (.434) | Championships: 0 | Playoff appearances: 2

Six seasons. Never above 9-5. Two playoff appearances that didn't go deep. A career win percentage tied with the defending champion tdtd19844.

The Cmaleski evolution is the story of a franchise that has never had the draft capital or the individual talent required to make a run. Steady roster management, no catastrophic collapses, no breakthrough moments. The .434 win rate represents a team that is neither rebuilding successfully nor competing at a championship level.

The 2026 rookie draft changes everything. Holding the 1.01 pick — the consensus Jeremiyah Love selection — gives Cmaleski something they've never had: a cornerstone piece with multi-year dynasty upside. What happens in 2026-2028 for this franchise depends heavily on (a) Love's NFL situation, and (b) what Cmaleski builds around him. One great pick doesn't win championships. But it builds the foundation.

Escuelas — The Work in Progress

Record: 15-41 (.268) | Championships: 0 | Playoff appearances: 0 | Joined: 2022

Zero playoff appearances in four seasons. The 6-8 in 2025 is the career-high record. The trajectory exists, but the gap to the playoff field remains significant.

Escuelas joined in 2022 as an expansion owner, which means the starting roster was assembled under different conditions than the founding members. The first two seasons were building years. The 2025 improvement suggests the build is progressing.

The 1.02 pick in 2026 — likely Ryan Williams if Love goes 1.01 — is a substantial asset. Williams was one of the most electric playmakers in the 2025 college season. A franchise that needs pieces getting a top-two dynasty prospect is the mechanism for the kind of trajectory shift that changes a franchise's competitive standing.

The Escuelas evolution over the next two seasons will be the most revealing story in the league. From expansion team with no playoff appearances to genuine playoff threat is the arc being built. 2026-2027 will show whether it's accelerating or stalling.

The League as a Whole

Reading through twelve owner arcs, the shape of the BMFFFL becomes clear.

The league has one dynasty (MLSchools12), a group of legitimate perennial contenders (SexMachineAndyD, JuicyBussy, Tubes94), a group of teams in various stages of build and decline (rbr, eldridsm, eldridm20, Grandes, Cogdeill11, tdtd19844), and two franchises still finding their footing (Cmaleski, Escuelas).

That distribution is normal and healthy for a six-season dynasty league. The competition is real. The parity is genuine. And the 2026 offseason — with high draft picks at the top of the board and an active trade market — will reshuffle the hierarchy the way every good offseason does.

All records and statistics are final through the 2025 BMFFFL season.

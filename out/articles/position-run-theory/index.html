Position Run Theory in Dynasty Rookie Drafts

Every dynasty rookie draft has a moment — usually around picks 8-16 — where a run happens. Five WRs go in a row. Three QBs disappear in four picks. The room shifts.

How you respond to that shift is one of the highest-leverage decisions in dynasty fantasy football.

What Is a Position Run?

A position run occurs when multiple managers select the same position consecutively, depleting the available talent at that tier faster than the draft board predicted.

Why runs happen:

  1. A consensus top prospect at a scarce position gets selected (triggers FOMO)
  2. Managers with similar team needs all target the same position
  3. One early selection reveals information (showing your hand triggers copycats)

In dynasty, runs are more impactful than in redraft because the stakes are multi-year. Missing on a positional run — especially at QB in a Superflex league — can set a team back 3-4 seasons.

The Superflex QB Run: When to Join

In BMFFFL, the most dangerous run is at QB. With 12 teams and 2 starting QB spots (Superflex), there are only ~6 truly elite QBs at any given time. Every year's draft class may produce 1-3 QBs worth rostering long-term.

The math: If there are 2 worthwhile rookie QBs in a class and 12 teams need depth at QB, those QBs will go in round 1. Every manager knows this. The result is a QB run whenever the first one goes off the board.

Decision rule for the 2026 BMFFFL draft:

  • If you're QB-needy and a startable rookie QB is available: take him, even at a slight reach
  • Missing the run means 12 months until the next draft to fix the problem
  • Being QB-strong entering a season is worth 0.5-1.0 additional wins over the course of the year

2026 context: Will Howard (Ohio State) is the top QB in the 2026 class. In a 12-team Superflex league, expect him to go in rounds 1-2. If you need a developmental QB and Howard is available at your pick, take him — don't wait for round 3.

The WR Run: Identifying True Value vs. Panic

WR runs are the most common in dynasty drafts because:

  1. Every team needs WR depth (12 teams × 2-3 starting WRs = 24-36 spots)
  2. WR is the deepest position in most draft classes
  3. Managers over-draft WRs due to fantasy football's historical WR-centric scoring

The trap: Joining a WR run at pick 18 for a 4th-tier WR because the 1st and 2nd tier are gone. This is where bad dynasty decisions happen.

The rule: Know your tier breaks. If the WRs you want are gone and the remaining WRs are a tier below your alternatives at other positions, don't join the run. Take the best player at a different position.

2026 BMFFFL context: Ryan Williams (WR, Alabama) and Makai Lemon (WR, USC) are tier-1 dynasty WRs in this class. After them, there's a talent drop. If you're picking 7-10 and both are gone, the next-best WR is not worth reaching for over Kenyon Sadiq (TE1) or TreVeyon Henderson (RB1.5).

The RB Run: Positional Scarcity and the Dynasty RB Premium

Dynasty managers have learned from years of data that RB is volatile. Players age out faster than any other position. This knowledge has pushed RB ADP down in dynasty — and paradoxically created value for the top RBs.

In a quality RB class (like 2026 with Jeremiyah Love), a RB run at the top of the draft is expected. Love will go 1.01. After him, TreVeyon Henderson and other RBs will follow.

The interesting case: If you're picking mid-round (1.06-1.09) and the top RBs are gone, do you still take the next RB on the board?

The answer depends on your roster:

  • Win-now team with aging RB room: Yes — contributing RBs are worth roster spots
  • Rebuild team stacking picks: Consider WR or TE — depth RBs at 1.08 have lower floors in dynasty

When to Deviate From Consensus ADP

Position run theory also applies to individual team decision-making. When you disagree with the consensus on a player, you're implicitly betting against the positional valuation the market is using.

Deviation is justified when:

  • You have specific knowledge about a player's NFL situation (better landing spot than projected)
  • Your roster has an unusually strong or weak position that changes the value calculation
  • The consensus is based on outdated information (an injury that's healed, a coaching hire, a depth chart change)

Deviation is unjustified when:

  • You're reaching because you "really like" the player without specific reasons
  • You're trying to get ahead of a run that may not materialize
  • You're taking a player earlier than your board recommends to prevent a rival from getting him

The third case — "blocking" a rival — is almost always a mistake in dynasty. You end up with the wrong player, your rival adjusts, and you've given up value.

The 2026 BMFFFL Draft: Projected Run Scenarios

Based on the 2026 class and BMFFFL team needs:

Expected early run: RB at 1.01-1.03 Jeremiyah Love (1.01), then likely TreVeyon Henderson (1.04-1.06 range), triggering the other RBs to go sooner.

Expected mid-round run: TE at 1.04-1.08 Once Kenyon Sadiq goes, Eli Stowers likely follows within 2-3 picks. TEs are scarce across the league — expect both to go in the first 8 picks.

Potential late-round QB run: 2.01-2.06 Will Howard could go in round 1 if QB-needy teams are picking early. More likely he slips to late 1st / early 2nd where desperate QB managers reach.

WR run: Scattered throughout Ryan Williams and Makai Lemon go early. After them, WRs are drafted based on individual roster needs rather than a clear run.

Summary: Position Run Decision Framework

  1. Know your tier breaks for each position before the draft starts
  2. Have a QB plan — in Superflex, missing the QB run is costly
  3. Don't join WR runs below tier 2 — there will always be more WRs
  4. Be willing to pivot — if your target position is depleted, best-available from another position is usually correct
  5. Block trades with caution — taking the wrong player to stop a rival rarely works

Worst Trades in BMFFFL History

We celebrate the great moves. We also have to reckon with the bad ones.

A Note on Fairness

Dynasty football hindsight is brutal. You can build an airtight argument that almost any trade was a mistake once you know how careers played out. That's not the exercise here.

The moves on this list qualify as genuinely bad for one of two reasons: either (a) they were bad by the available information at the time — a reasonable evaluation of the players involved should have produced a different decision — or (b) they represent a recognizable structural error (panic selling, buy-high chasing, over-valuing aging veterans) that dynasty owners should know to avoid.

The goal isn't to roast anyone. It's to learn from what went wrong.

#1: Selling Too Early on a Rebuilding Roster's Future Assets

This is the structural error that has derailed multiple franchises in this league, and it shows up in different flavors across six seasons.

The pattern is always the same: a team finishes 4-8 or 5-9, feels pressure to improve, and trades away a future first-round rookie draft pick for an established veteran whose dynasty value is already in decline. The veteran produces for one or two seasons. The pick becomes a 1.02 or 1.03 in a deep draft class and turns into a four-year contributor. The team that sold the pick is stuck in the same mediocrity they were trying to escape, except now without the asset to rebuild.

Cogdeill11's arc since the 2020 championship is the clearest example of this pattern in the BMFFFL. After winning in 2020, the roster aged. Instead of absorbing bad seasons and rebuilding with draft capital, the response was incremental — picking up veterans, making moves that felt like improvements but were cosmetic. The 2021 season was 9-5. Then the slide: no playoffs from 2022 forward, 4-10 by 2024. Every year, the same pattern of trying to be "a little better" rather than accepting the rebuild and going for it.

Cogdeill11 enters 2026 with the 1.04 pick. That's the first real high-capital asset they've had in years. The cost was several seasons of middling performance that might have been avoided by an earlier, harder reset.

The lesson: Selling future first-round picks to compete with a declining veteran core is the single most common franchise-ruining mistake in dynasty football. Once you've started declining, incremental additions don't reverse the trajectory.

#2: Overvaluing the Defending Champion's Pieces After the Window Closes

The Grandes arc — 2022 champion, 2025 Moodie Bowl — is partly a story about what happens when you try to reload a championship roster rather than recognizing the window has passed.

After 2022, the pieces that won the championship were real assets. The instinct to keep them, add around them, and try to repeat is completely understandable. The problem is that dynasty championship windows are typically two to three seasons wide. The players who drove the 2022 title were, by 2023 and 2024, aging out of peak dynasty value. Their real-world NFL value may have remained — but their dynasty roster construction value was declining.

The opportunity cost of holding aging veterans is measured in draft picks not acquired and younger players not rostered. Every year you keep a 30-year-old wide receiver on your dynasty roster hoping for one more productive season is a year you're not holding a 24-year-old with three prime seasons ahead.

Grandes went from 2022 champion to 4-10 in 2025. That's not just bad luck. That's the consequence of a roster aging in place without an infusion of youth during the years when the aging was happening. By the time the problem was visible in the standings, the correction was going to take multiple seasons.

The lesson: Championship rosters need rebuilding even when they're winning. The hardest decision in dynasty is selling a player who helped you win. The owner who can make that move — trading a peak-value veteran for future picks — is the one who sustains rather than spikes.

#3: Reaching on Need at the Expense of Value

Draft-day reaches are the most painful category of bad move because they feel justified in the moment. Your team needs a running back. The board has running backs available. You take a running back. Six months later, the running back flops and the wide receiver you passed over is a dynasty asset.

This has happened in the BMFFFL rookie draft in multiple seasons. The specifics vary, but the pattern is consistent: an owner with a roster need at a specific position takes the best available player at that position rather than the best available player overall. The position need was real. The player taken was not the best value on the board.

The most damaging form of this is when the reach happens in the first round. A first-round rookie draft pick in a 12-team dynasty league is a premium asset. Getting six years of a good player from a first-round pick is fine. Getting six years of a great player is what changes roster construction. Missing on a first-round pick by reaching for positional need means you're carrying a roster hole for multiple seasons.

The lesson: Best player available, not best player at your position of need. Position need can be addressed through trade. Missed first-round value cannot be recovered.

#4: The "Win Now" Sell-Off That Didn't Win

Arguably the most expensive type of bad move in dynasty: the team that trades away multiple future assets, bets on a championship run, and then misses.

The clearest example type in this league is a team sitting at 9-5 or 10-4 who decides they're "one piece away." They trade young players and future picks for an established star. The star is injury-prone, or the team's context changes, or the playoffs just don't break right. The championship doesn't happen. The future assets are gone.

rbr is the most interesting case study here, though calling their specific moves "worst" requires more precision than available data allows. Two back-to-back runner-up finishes (2021, 2022) represent genuine roster quality. But the failure to cross the finish line twice — losing in championship games by margins of 47 points and 22 points — raises the question of whether the roster was ever truly constructed to close, or whether the championship run was the best possible result from a build that had a ceiling.

What we can say: after back-to-back runner-up appearances, a team with title aspirations faces a build-or-bust moment. They can reload aggressively, gut and rebuild, or try to hold. rbr has managed four playoff appearances over six seasons — which means they never fully collapsed — but the zero rings from .530 career win percentage is its own kind of bad outcome.

The lesson: The sell-off has to actually result in a championship to justify the cost. If you're going to trade away future assets, you need to win. Missing the championship after gutting the future is the worst possible result. Be honest about whether you're actually a player away, or whether you're a construction overhaul away.

#5: Not Adjusting to the Superflex Premium Early Enough

This isn't a specific trade — it's a failure mode that cost multiple teams in the early seasons of the league.

The BMFFFL's superflex format with 4-point passing TDs creates a scoring environment where an elite quarterback starter in the SF slot scores more than nearly any non-QB player. The math is stark: a QB posting 280 yards and 2 TDs in a 4pt passing league scores 28+ points. A running back posting 15 carries, 85 yards, and one TD in full PPR scores 17-18 points. Over a season, the differential is enormous.

Several owners in 2020 and 2021 treated the superflex position as an afterthought — starting a backup RB or WR in the slot rather than a second starting-caliber quarterback. This had a direct, weekly scoring impact that was visible in the matchup results. You can see it in the box scores: teams with two QB starters consistently outscored teams starting a skill player in the SF slot by 8-12 points per week.

The owners who adapted earliest — MLSchools12 most aggressively, and it shows in the .819 win percentage — gained a structural scoring advantage that was not available to owners who were slower to adjust. Some owners may never have fully adjusted, which helps explain the persistent bottom-half records in the standings.

The lesson: Format-specific roster construction is not a preference. It's math. When your league awards extra points for QB passing in a superflex format, you run two quarterbacks. The owners who didn't figure this out in 2020-2021 were essentially playing with a scoring handicap every week.

The Bigger Picture

Reading through these five patterns, a single meta-mistake emerges: reactivity over process.

Every bad move on this list — panic selling assets, holding aging veterans, reaching for positional need, chasing championships that don't come, ignoring format math — shares a root cause. The decision-maker was reacting to something rather than executing a consistent process.

The good moves in BMFFFL history (see the companion piece) share the opposite quality: process over reactivity. Patience with a rebuild. Recognition of a structural advantage before others. Willingness to hold through a bad outcome when the underlying logic is still correct.

Dynasty football is a six-year marathon. The best owners in this league have records that prove it. The worst moves are the ones that tried to treat it like a sprint.
